### Slide: Innovating at speed for the enterprise

**Slide text:**
> What enterprise customers need
> Most of our customers aren't individuals trying a chatbot. They're admins answering for thousands of seats — and they need to feel safe before they say yes.

- Admin controls — gated rollout, kill switches
- Transparency of cost — what am I paying for, per-feature, per-seat?
- Clear data boundaries — where does it go, what's used for training?
- PII protection by default, not as a setting
- Outputs reviewed before they reach production
- A clear way to turn it off — for compliance, for incidents, for trust

_This is the unsolved tension: ship at AI speed, or earn enterprise trust. We have to do both._

**Speaker notes:**
Designers have a responsibility to do this thinking. There's real tension between speed and responsibility. Name that tension — it's an unsolved problem. The industry is moving so fast that expectations and available technology shift within days. Enterprise customers need thoughtfulness, transparency, and control — and those things take time.

### Slide: Where are you on the curve?

**Slide text:**
> Where are you on the curve?
> Most organisations sit at the early stages — experimenting, adopting. A few have made it to integration. The leap from one stage to the next is cultural, not technical — and designers can lead it.

- Notion describes five stages of team AI transformation — from dormant, to exploring, adopting, integrating, and finally reinventing the way work happens

**Speaker notes:**
Use this as a mirror for the audience. Ask: "Where is your team?" Most are still in Exploring or Adopting. The Atlassian story is about pushing through to Integrated and Strategic. The leap is cultural, not technical — and that's why design leadership matters here.

### Slide: Ma

**Slide text:**
> Ma
> The Japanese concept of the space between. Not absence — presence.

"Ma is a metaphor for how we need to structure our attention. We need to design our UX to avoid the rabbit hole, slow down, and see things more clearly."
— Scott Jenson, The Ma of a New Machine (2026)

**Speaker notes:**
Ma is the concept of negative space in Japanese aesthetics — the pause between notes, the breath before the next move. Scott Jenson recently wrote about Ma as the antidote to the "epistemic rabbit hole" of chatbots: fast AI that pulls you deeper without reflection. His point: design interfaces that naturally invite the pause. Reference the Tadao Ando architecture image — the interplay of concrete and light in Naoshima is a perfect visual analog of ma in built space.

### Slide: Hands in the clay _(section header)_

**Slide text:**
> Hands in the clay

**Speaker notes:**
End on the metaphor. Designers should put their hands in the clay. Use the tools. Stay in the material. The real unlock: being present right at the end, in the actual thing, catching what only a designer would catch — and increasingly able to fix it themselves. From the idealised upstream version of the thing, to the actual thing, right before it meets a customer. That's where the craft relocates. Summary: put your hands in the clay. Understand what the material can actually do. Close the gap between what you design and what ships. That's the job now.
